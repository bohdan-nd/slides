# Foundations of Flow Matching

# Flow Matching, General Idea

**Goal**: generate samples from a complex distribution $p_{\text{data}}$ (images, videos, molecules, ...) that we only access through examples

**Idea**: learn to *continuously transform* a simple distribution (e.g. Gaussian noise) into the data distribution over time $t \in [0, 1]$

<div class="note">

$$
\underbrace{p_0 = \mathcal{N}(0, I)}_{\text{easy to sample}} \;\;\xrightarrow{\;\;\text{learned transport}\;\;}\;\; \underbrace{p_1 = p_{\text{data}}}_{\text{what we want}}
$$

</div>

<img :src="'./figures/noise_to_data.gif?v=2'" class="mx-auto block" style="height: 220px; width: auto;" />

---
class: compact
cite: 'Naida &amp; Nguyen, <em>Flow Matching &amp; GRPO</em>'
---

# Ordinary Differential Equations

<div class="definition">

An **ODE** is an equation connecting an unknown function $X : I \to \mathbb{R}^d$ to its derivatives, where $I \subseteq \mathbb{R}$ is an interval. A **first-order ODE** has the form
$$
\frac{dX_t}{dt} = f(X_t,\, t), \qquad f : \mathbb{R}^d \times I \to \mathbb{R}^d.
$$

</div>

<div class="definition">

A **solution**, called a **trajectory**, is a differentiable function $X : I \to \mathbb{R}^d,\; t \mapsto X_t$ that satisfies the ODE pointwise.

</div>

**Why first-order?** In flow matching we specify velocities directly; this is what the neural network outputs, so first-order is the right level of description. We take $I = [0, 1]$ and interpret $t$ as time.

<img :src="'./figures/ode_trajectory.gif'" class="mx-auto block" style="height: 130px; width: auto;" />

<div class="example">

A water particle flowing downstream: at each time $t$, $X_t$ gives its position in the river.

</div>

---
class: compact
cite: 'Naida &amp; Nguyen, <em>Flow Matching &amp; GRPO</em>'
---

# Vector Field

<div class="definition">

A **vector field** is a function that, for every time $t$ and location $x$, returns a vector $u_t(x) \in \mathbb{R}^d$ specifying a velocity in space:
$$
u : \mathbb{R}^d \times I \to \mathbb{R}^d, \qquad (x, t) \mapsto u_t(x).
$$

</div>

<div class="vf-row">
<div class="vf-left">

Every vector field defines an **ODE**:

- $\dot{X}_t = u_t(X_t)$: the trajectory follows the field
- $X_0 = x_0$: the trajectory starts at an initial point

</div>
<div class="vf-right">

<img :src="'./figures/vector_field.gif?v=2'" style="height: 320px; width: auto;" />

</div>
</div>

---
layout: two-cols
class: compact
cite: 'Naida &amp; Nguyen, <em>Flow Matching &amp; GRPO</em>'
---

# Flow

::left::

<div class="definition">

A **flow** is the rule that tells you where every starting point ends up after a given amount of time, when you let it move according to a differential equation
$$
\Psi : \mathbb{R}^d \times [0, 1] \to \mathbb{R}^d, \qquad \Psi_t(x_0) = \text{position at time } t \text{ if you started at } x_0.
$$

It is itself a solution of the ODE, one curve for every starting point:
$$
\tfrac{d}{dt} \Psi_t(x_0) = u_t(\Psi_t(x_0)), \quad \Psi_0(x_0) = x_0.
$$

</div>

For any initial condition $X_0 = x_0$, the corresponding trajectory is recovered by fixing $x_0$: $X_t = \Psi_t(x_0)$

While a trajectory is a single curve, the flow tracks all of them at once. The flow transports distributions, not just points

::right::

<img :src="'./figures/flow.gif'" class="mx-auto block" style="width: 100%; height: auto; max-height: 360px;" />

<p style="text-align: center; font-size: 0.8rem; color: #6E6967; margin-top: 0.4em;">
<span style="color: #1f77b4; font-weight: 700;">■</span> flow Ψ<sub>t</sub> (warped grid) &nbsp;&nbsp;
<span style="color: #f4c20d; font-weight: 700;">→</span> velocity field u<sub>t</sub>
</p>

cite: 'Naida &amp; Nguyen, <em>Flow Matching &amp; GRPO</em>'

# From Flows to Flow Matching

In **flow matching** we re-use the tools above to transport noise into data on $I = [0, 1]$:

- A **trajectory** $X_t$ is the path of a single noisy sample as it is transformed into a data sample
- The **vector field** $u_t$ is what we learn: a neural network trained to point toward data
- The **flow** $\Psi_t$ carries *every* noise point to a data point at once; it is the generative map

<img :src="'./figures/noise_to_data_vector.gif'" class="mx-auto block" style="height: 300px; width: auto;" />

---
class: compact roomy
cite: 'Naida &amp; Nguyen, <em>Flow Matching &amp; GRPO</em>'

# Interpolation Schedules & Training

We need paths connecting noise $x_0 \sim p_0$ to data $x_1 \sim p_{\text{data}}$

A general construction: $x_t = \alpha_t\, x_0 + \beta_t\, x_1$, with the schedule $(\alpha_t, \beta_t)$ chosen so that $x_0$ is pure noise and $x_1$ is pure data. The most common choice:

<div class="note">

- **Linear**: $\alpha_t = 1-t,\ \beta_t = t$ (straight line from $x_0$ to $x_1$).

</div>

Each point $x_t$ has a well-defined velocity $u_t(x_t \mid x_0, x_1) = \dot{\alpha}_t\, x_0 + \dot{\beta}_t\, x_1$.

For the linear schedule:$\dot{\alpha}_t = -1$ and $\dot{\beta}_t = 1$, so this simplifies to $x_1 - x_0$.

To train, sample $x_0 \sim p_0,\ x_1 \sim p_{\text{data}},\ t \sim \mathcal{U}[0, 1]$, form $x_t$, and regress the network's predicted velocity onto the path velocity:

<div class="note">

$$
\mathcal{L}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}\bigl[\,\|u_t^{\theta}(x_t) - (x_1 - x_0)\|^2\,\bigr]
$$

</div>

**Intuition**: Each example sees only one noise data pair, but in expectation the network learns the *marginal* velocity at $x_t$ the average direction transporting noise into data

<div class="terminology">

In the diffusion literature this is usually called a **noise schedule**, parameterized as $x_t = \alpha_t\, x_1 + \sigma_t\, \varepsilon$ with $\varepsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I)$.

</div>

# Discrete-State Diffusion

**The challenge**: Text lives in a discrete token space. The continuous FM/diffusion tools does not transfer directly

**The idea**: Define the forward process *directly* on tokens

<div class="definition">

**Masking diffusion**: The forward process replaces each token independently by `[MASK]` according to the **probability**:
$$
q(x_t^i \mid x_0^i) \;=\; \alpha_t\,\mathbb{1}[x_t^i = x_0^i] \;+\; (1-\alpha_t)\,\mathbb{1}[x_t^i = \texttt{[MASK]}].
$$
Reverse process iteratively unmasks the sentence

</div>

<div class="note">

**Not smooth.** Unlike continuous flow smooth ODE trajectories, the state is **piecewise constant**. Each token holds its value until it jumps to `[MASK]`, driven by a jump rate rather than a velocity field

</div>

<img :src="'./figures/jump_vs_smooth.gif'" class="mx-auto block" style="height: 220px; width: auto; margin-top: 0.4em;" />

class: compact roomy

# Joint Generation: What's Missing

A joint model over a continuous modality (images or 3D coords) and a discrete one (tokens, atom types):

- **No shared latent space** Continuous side lives in $\mathbb{R}^d$ with a velocity $u_t(x)$; discrete side lives on a finite alphabet with a rate matrix $Q_t$

- **No aligned schedules** "Half-noised image" and "half-masked text" measure different things; the joint schedule becomes a design choice

- **No unified sampler** Continuous and Discrete need different types of samplers

- **No gradual reveal** Tokens flip from `[MASK]` to a real value in one shot

<div class="example">

Generate a photo of an animal (continuous pixels) jointly with its caption (discrete tokens, masked)

At $t = 0.3$ the caption is fully `[MASK]` and the half-formed image is drifting toward *some* cat-like features. When `dog` unmasks at $t = 0.5$, the image has to *self-correct*. With a continuous text embedding, a noisy "dog-ish" signal would have been present from the start, and early pixel choices could already be steered towards an image of a dog
</div>

---
class: compact roomy
---

# Continuous Diffusion over Discrete Data

Keep using continuous-space tools by *representing* tokens as continuous vectors first.

<div class="note">

1. **Represent** each token as a continuous vectors
2. **Work** in this space, same ODE/SDE
3. **Decode** back to tokens at the end

</div>

**Usual**: Convert each token to a **one-hot vector** and run flow matching / diffusion on the **vocabulary simplex** $\Delta^{|V|-1}$. Decoding is then $\arg\max$ over the coordinates

<img :src="'./figures/simplex_3tokens.png'" class="mx-auto block" style="height: 230px; width: auto; margin-top: 0.4em;" />

# Interpolants & Training

**Choice of interpolant**: A continuous path on $\Delta^{|V|-1}$ from a noise prior $x_0$ to a clean one-hot $x_1$ over $t \in [0, 1]$:

<div class="note">

- **Linear**: $x_t = (1-t)\, x_0 + t\, x_1$ (the default)
- **Dirichlet**: $x_t \sim \mathrm{Dir}(\alpha(t, x_1))$

</div>

**Training** (linear case). Sample $x_0 \sim p_{\text{noise}}$, $x_1 \sim p_{\text{data}}$ (one-hot), $t \sim \mathcal{U}[0, 1]$, form $x_t$, and regress the network's velocity onto the path velocity:

<div class="note">

$$
u_t(x_t \mid x_0, x_1) \;=\; x_1 - x_0,
\qquad
\mathcal{L}_{\text{FM}}(\theta) \;=\; \mathbb{E}\bigl[\,\lVert v_\theta(x_t, t) - (x_1 - x_0) \rVert^2\,\bigr]
$$

</div>

<div class="terminology">

**In practice: predict the clean data instead.** The velocity lives in a very high-dimensional space ($|V|$ is tens of thousands), so regressing on it directly is hard. Predicting $x_1$ is much easier, so train a **denoiser / classifier** $\hat{x}_1^\theta(x_t, t)$ with cross-entropy, and **recover the velocity** at inference:
$$
v_\theta(x_t, t) \;=\; \frac{\hat{x}_1^\theta(x_t, t) \;-\; x_t}{1 - t}
$$

</div>

**Sampling**: Start at $x_0 \sim p_{\text{noise}}$. At each step, run the denoiser, plug into the formula above to get $v_\theta$, take the step

# Joint State Space

Concatenate 3D coordinates $\mathbb{R}^3$, atom types on $\Delta^{|A|-1}$, and text tokens on $\Delta^{|V|-1}$ to put the full joint state in $\mathbb{R}^3 \times \Delta^{|A|-1} \times \Delta^{|V|-1}$, an affine subspace *parallel* to the simplex. Joint flow matching now runs on a *single* continuous space:

<img :src="'./figures/simplex_prism.gif'" class="mx-auto block" style="height: 220px; width: auto; margin-top: 0.4em;" />

<div class="example">

- **Blue triangle** (vertices A, B, C): the **token simplex** $\Delta^{|V|-1}$ for $|V| = 3$ tokens.
- **Spatial axis**: in real molecule generation this is $\mathbb{R}^3$; *here collapsed to 1D*
- **Blue prism**: the **joint state space**, the token simplex *replicated* along the spatial axis
- **Red dot** is a single sample of **molecule** combined with **text**. Its $x$-coordinate is the molecule's spatial position; and $(y, z)$ is the text's token distribution

**During the flow** the spatial coordinate unboundedly slides freely along the axis, while the token coordinate stays *always inside the simplex*, the dot never leaves the prism

</div>

---
class: compact
cite: 'Xu et al., <em>Geometric Latent Diffusion Models</em>'

# GeoLDM: Encoding the Molecule

**Problem**: Raw atoms (3D coords + categorical type) are not equivariant to rotation and translation, mix continuous and discrete

An **E(3)-equivariant encoder** maps each atom to a *point-structured latent*:

<div class="note">

- **Invariant scalars** ($\in \mathbb{R}^6$): 5 atom types (one-hot over $\{\text{H, C, N, O, F}\}$) + 1 partial charge
- **Equivariant tensors** ($\in \mathbb{R}^3$): the atom's 3D coordinates

</div>

**Benefits:**

- *Equivariance* The encoder is rotation and translation-equivariant, so any model operating on its latents inherits that symmetry
- *Decoder* A matching equivariant decoder reconstructs atom types and 3D coordinates at the end

**Pipeline**:

(atom types, 3D coords) $\to$ GeoLDM encoder $\to$ atom latents $\to$ **joint FM** $\to$ atom latents $\to$ GeoLDM decoder $\to$ (atom types, 3D coords)

# Diffusion Transformer (DiT)

::left::

<img :src="'./figures/dit.png'" style="width: 100%; height: auto; max-height: 380px;" />

<p style="text-align: center; font-size: 0.75rem; color: #6E6967; margin-top: 0.4em;">
DiT block: AdaLN-modulated attention + MLP.
</p>

::right::

DiT adapted to the joint state. Atoms are first encoded via **GeoLDM** into a latent space (invariant scalars + equivariant tensors)

<div class="note">

- **Input sequence**: text tokens (each on $\Delta^{|V|-1}$) interleaved with atom latents from the GeoLDM encoder
- **Prediction targets**: predict the clean $\hat{x}_1$ for tokens (denoiser, softmax); predict the **added noise** for the atom latents (MSE).
- **Time conditioning**: a sinusoidal embedding of $t$ controls the Adaptive Layer Norm's scale and shift inside every block.

</div>

**Why DiT** One backbone for both modalities, no separate text or molecule networks. Attention couples atoms and tokens directly. GeoLDM gives equivariance for free, so the DiT itself doesn't need to be E(3)-equivariant

# Three Equivalent Parameterisations

For the linear interpolant $x_t = (1-t)\,x_0 + t\,x_1$, the network can be trained to predict **any of three quantities**. They are algebraically equivalent under a linear change of variables, but their *regression targets* have very different structure, so they train very differently

<div class="note">

| name | network outputs | regression target |
|---|---|---|
| **Velocity** (FM) | $v_\theta(x_t, t)$ | $x_1 - x_0$ |
| **Noise** ($\varepsilon$-pred) | $\varepsilon_\theta(x_t, t)$ | $x_0$ |
| **Denoiser** ($x_1$-pred) | $\hat{x}_1^\theta(x_t, t)$ | $x_1$ |

</div>

**Relationships.** Any one fixes the others by a linear change of variables:
$$
v = x_1 - x_0, \quad x_1 = x_t + (1-t)\,v, \quad x_0 = x_t - t\,v.
$$

**Sampling is parameterisation-agnostic**: Given any of the three predictions at $x_t$, the others follow analytically and the ODE step is the same

# The Project

Malte modelled an **edit-flows-based** generative model for 3D molecules. Three edit actions:

<div class="note">

- **Add** a new atom
- **Change** an existing atom's type
- **Delete** an atom

</div>

We are extending it to text, so it can be conditioned on language for:

- *Goal-oriented design*: generate molecules having some properties or goals defines in text
- *Reasoning about 3D structures*: describe and explain a generated structure in natural language
- *Textual edits*: rewrite a molecule by textual commands

**Reasoning-based action selection.** Vu and I are exploring choosing the next edit action via reasoning rather than direct sampling

Building a molecule takes orders of magnitude fewer edit steps than in dLLMs, so reasoning at every step is much more feasible here, but still computationally expensive
